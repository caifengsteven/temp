Multivariate Quadratic Hawkes Processes: Detailed Mathematical Analysis

1. Introduction and Motivation

1.1 Empirical Evidence

The paper is motivated by two key empirical observations in financial markets:

Cross-Asset Leverage Effect

Correlation between asset i's volatility and asset j's past returns:

$$\text{Corr}\left(\sigma^2_{i,t}, R_{j,t-\tau}\right) \neq 0$$

where \(\sigma^2_{i,t}\) is the volatility of asset i and \(R_{j,t-\tau}\) is the return of asset j at lag \(\tau\).

Cross-Zumbach Effect

Asymmetric correlation between volatility and squared returns:

$$\text{Corr}\left(\sigma^2_{i,t}, R^2_{j,t-\tau}\right) \neq \text{Corr}\left(\sigma^2_{i,t}, R^2_{j,t+\tau}\right)$$

This time-reversal asymmetry indicates that past trends affect future volatility differently than future trends affect past volatility.

2. Model Definition

2.1 Multivariate Quadratic Hawkes (MQHawkes) Process

General MQHawkes Intensity

For N financial assets with prices \((P^i_t)_{t \geq 0, i=1,...,N}\), the intensity of asset i is:

$$\lambda^i_t = \lambda^i_\infty + \sum_{j=1}^N \int_{-\infty}^t L^i_j(t-s) dP^j_s + \sum_{j \leq k}^N \int_{-\infty}^t \int_{-\infty}^t K^i_{jk}(t-s, t-u) dP^j_s dP^k_u$$

where:

  • \(\lambda^i_\infty\): Baseline intensity for asset i
  • \(L^i_j(t-s)\): Leverage kernel measuring impact of asset j's returns on asset i's activity
  • \(K^i_{jk}(t-s, t-u)\): Quadratic kernel for trend effects from assets j and k

2.2 ZHawkes Specification

Kernel Decomposition

The quadratic kernels are decomposed as:

$$K^i_{jj}(s,u) = \underbrace{\phi^i_{jj}(s)\delta(s-u)}_{\text{Time-diagonal (Hawkes)}} + \underbrace{k^i_{jj}(s)k^i_{jj}(u)}_{\text{Rank-one (Zumbach)}}$$

For cross-terms (i < j):

$$K^i_{ij}(s,u) = \phi^i_{ij}(s)\delta(s-u) + k^i_{ji}(s)k^i_{ij}(u)$$

Resulting ZHawkes Model

$$\lambda^i_t = \lambda^i_\infty + \sum_{j} \int_{-\infty}^t L^i_j(t-s) dP^j_s + \sum_{j} \int_{-\infty}^t \phi^i_{jj}(t-s) dN^j_s + \sum_{j} \left[\int_{-\infty}^t k^i_{jj}(t-s) dP^j_s\right]^2$$

3. Endogeneity and Stationarity

3.1 Mean Intensity

Mean Intensity Vector

When finite, the mean intensity vector satisfies:

$$\bar{\lambda} = \left(I - \int_0^\infty K_d(s,s)ds\right)^{-1} \lambda_\infty$$

where \(K_d\) is the matrix of diagonal quadratic kernels with \((K_d)_{ij} = K^i_{jj}\).

3.2 Endogeneity Ratios

Hawkes Endogeneity

$$n_H = \rho_{\text{spectral}}\left(\|\phi_d\|\right)$$

Spectral radius of the Hawkes kernel matrix

Total Endogeneity

$$n = \rho_{\text{spectral}}\left(\|K_d\|\right)$$

Spectral radius of the full diagonal kernel

Stationarity Conditions

There exists a critical value \(n^*\) such that:

  • If \(n_H < 1\) and \(n < n^*\): Stationary with finite mean intensity
  • If \(n_H < 1\) and \(n > n^*\): Stationary with infinite mean intensity
  • If \(n_H > 1\): Process explodes

4. Modeling Co-jumps

4.1 Correlated Poisson Processes

Price Process with Co-jumps

To allow simultaneous jumps across assets:

$$\begin{aligned} dP^1_t &= \epsilon^1_t (dN^1_t + dN^c_t) \\ dP^2_t &= \epsilon^2_t (dN^2_t + dN^c_t) \end{aligned}$$

where:

  • \(N^1_t, N^2_t\): Idiosyncratic jump processes
  • \(N^c_t\): Common jump process
  • \(\epsilon^i_t = \pm 1\): Random signs with \(\mathbb{E}[\epsilon^1_t \epsilon^2_t] = \rho\)

Co-jump Product

$$dP^1_t dP^2_t = \epsilon^1_t \epsilon^2_t dN^c_t$$

Non-zero only when both assets jump simultaneously via \(dN^c_t\).

5. Covariance Structure and Yule-Walker Equations

5.1 Correlation Functions

Two-point Covariance

$$C_{ij}(\tau) = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{dN^i_t}{dt} \frac{dN^j_{t-\tau}}{dt}\right] - \bar{\lambda}^i\bar{\lambda}^j$$

Measures activity correlation between assets i and j

Three-point Correlation

$$D_{ijk}(\tau_1, \tau_2) = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{dN^i_t}{dt} - \bar{\lambda}^i\right) \frac{dP^j_{t-\tau_1}}{dt} \frac{dP^k_{t-\tau_2}}{dt}\right]$$

Captures trend effects on activity

5.2 Yule-Walker Equations

Two-point Yule-Walker Equation

$$C(\tau) = K_d(\tau)\lambda + \int_0^\infty K_d(u,u)C(\tau-u)du + 2\int_0^\infty \int_u^\infty K_d(\tau+u, \tau+r)D_d(u,r)dr\,du + \text{cross terms}$$

This matrix equation relates observable covariances \(C(\tau)\) to the kernels \(K_d\).

Three-point Yule-Walker Equation

For \(\tau_1 > \tau_2 > 0\):

$$D_d(\tau_1, \tau_2) = 2K_d(\tau_1, \tau_2)C_d(\tau_2-\tau_1) + \int_{\tau_1}^\infty K_d(u)D_d(\tau_1-u, \tau_2-u)du + \text{additional terms}$$

6. Volatility Distribution and Power-Law Tails

6.1 Fokker-Planck Equation

Continuous-Time Limit

The probability density \(p_t(h_1, h_2, z_1, z_2)\) evolves according to:

$$\begin{aligned} \partial_t p_t = &\beta_1\partial_{h_1}[(h_1-\lambda_{1,t})p_t] + \beta_2\partial_{h_2}[(h_2-\lambda_{2,t})p_t] \\ &+ \omega_1\partial_{z_1}(z_1 p_t) + \omega_2\partial_{z_2}(z_2 p_t) \\ &+ \frac{\omega_1^2}{2}\partial^2_{z_1z_1}(\lambda_{1,t} p_t) + \frac{\omega_2^2}{2}\partial^2_{z_2z_2}(\lambda_{2,t} p_t) \end{aligned}$$

6.2 Power-Law Tail Behavior

Asymptotic Distribution

In polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\) with \(z_1 = r\cos\theta\), \(z_2 = r\sin\theta\):

$$p_\infty(r,\theta) \underset{r \to \infty}{\sim} F(\theta) r^{-\alpha}$$

where:

  • \(\alpha\): Universal tail exponent (same for all assets)
  • \(F(\theta)\): Angular distribution function

6.3 Tail Exponent Results

Isotropic Case (\(\gamma = 0\))

$$\alpha_0 = 2 + \frac{1}{n_Z}$$

No cross-asset effects

General Case

$$\alpha = \alpha_0 + \frac{\gamma^2}{32}\left(\frac{4}{n_Z} - \frac{1}{n_Z^3}\right) + O(\gamma^4)$$

Cross-effects modify the exponent

Limiting Behaviors

Large \(n_Z\) limit:
$$\alpha \to 2$$

Maximally fat tails

Small \(n_Z\) limit:
$$\alpha \to \infty$$

Thin tails (Gaussian-like)

7. Analytical Methods

7.1 Schrödinger-like Equation for Tail Exponent

ODE for Angular Distribution

The function \(F(\theta)\) satisfies:

$$[(\alpha-2)(\alpha-\alpha_0) + (\alpha-2)/2 \cdot \gamma\cos(\theta)\sin(\theta)/F(\theta) + [(1+\gamma\cos(\theta)\sin(\theta))F(\theta)]''] = 0$$

Subject to:

  • \(F(\theta) \geq 0\) everywhere
  • \(F(\theta + \pi) = F(\theta)\) (periodicity)

7.2 Critical Point for Mean Intensity

Finite Mean Condition

The mean intensity is finite when:

$$n_Z < n^* \approx 1 + \frac{\gamma^2}{8} + O(\gamma^4)$$

For the isotropic case (\(\gamma = 0\)): \(n^* = 1\)

8. Key Results and Implications

8.1 Main Findings

Universal Tail Exponent

All assets in the system share the same power-law tail exponent \(\alpha\), suggesting a fundamental mechanism for tail universality in financial markets.

Role of Quadratic Feedback

Even small Zumbach feedback (\(n_Z \ll 1\)) coupled with near-critical Hawkes feedback (\(n_H \approx 1\)) generates realistic fat tails.

8.2 Practical Calibration Strategy

Calibration Steps

  1. Estimate empirical covariances \(C_{ij}(\tau)\) from data
  2. Estimate three-point correlations \(D_{ijk}(\tau_1, \tau_2)\)
  3. Solve Yule-Walker equations for kernels \(L^i_j\), \(K^i_{jk}\)
  4. Verify stationarity: \(n_H < 1\), \(n < n^*\)
  5. Extract tail exponent \(\alpha\) from calibrated parameters

The framework can accommodate time-varying parameters to capture different market regimes (normal, crisis, recovery).

While detailed analysis focuses on N=2, the framework extends to arbitrary N assets with increasing computational complexity.
