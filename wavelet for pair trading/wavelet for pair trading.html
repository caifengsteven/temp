Pairs Trading with Wavelet Transform:
A Comprehensive Analysis

Authors: Burak Alparslan Eroğlu, Haluk Yener, Taner Yiğit

Journal: Quantitative Finance, 2023

Key Innovation: Using wavelet transform to filter noise from stock prices dramatically improves pairs trading performance

Executive Summary

Core Problem and Solution

Pairs trading profitability has been declining, especially after 2009. This paper demonstrates that noise in stock prices is the main culprit, and filtering prices with wavelet transform before pairs trading leads to:

  • Dramatic return improvement: From -1.81% to +9.66% (cointegration method)
  • Higher Sharpe ratios: From -0.40 to 2.82 average
  • Better risk profile: Positive skewness instead of negative
  • More successful trades: Convergent trades increase from 12% to 31%

Why This Matters

Pairs trading is a market-neutral strategy that should work regardless of market direction. However, common noise in stock prices (market-wide movements, sector effects) can mask true price relationships, leading to:

  • Poor pair selection (false positives due to common noise)
  • Inaccurate parameter estimation (measurement error problem)
  • Non-stationary spreads in the trading period
  • Large losses from non-convergent trades

1. Understanding Pairs Trading

1.1 Basic Concept

Pairs trading involves:

  1. Finding related stocks: Two stocks that historically move together
  2. Identifying divergence: When their prices temporarily diverge
  3. Trading the spread: Buy the undervalued, short the overvalued
  4. Profit from convergence: Close positions when prices converge

1.2 Two Main Methods

Minimum Distance Method

Find pairs with smallest historical price distance:

$$D_{i,j} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} (\tilde{S}_{i,t} - \tilde{S}_{j,t})^2$$

where $\tilde{S}$ are normalized prices

  • Simple and intuitive
  • Based on "law of one price"
  • No statistical assumptions

Cointegration Method

Find pairs with long-run equilibrium:

$$S_{i,t} = \alpha + \beta S_{j,t} + \epsilon_t$$

where $\epsilon_t$ is stationary

  • Statistically rigorous
  • Tests for mean reversion
  • Johansen test at 5% significance

1.3 The Trading Process

Spread Definition and Trading Rules

Standard Spread: $\hat{s}_t = S_{i,t} - \hat{\alpha} - \hat{\beta}S_{j,t}$

Trading Signals:

  • Open long spread: when $\hat{s}_t < -2\sigma$ (buy stock i, short $\beta$ of stock j)
  • Open short spread: when $\hat{s}_t > 2\sigma$ (short stock i, buy $\beta$ of stock j)
  • Close position: when spread crosses zero or changes sign
  • Force close: at end of trading period

2. Wavelet Transform: The Key Innovation

2.1 What is Wavelet Transform?

Time-Frequency Decomposition

Wavelets decompose time series into different frequency components:

  • Low-frequency (long-run): Trends and fundamental relationships
  • High-frequency (short-run): Noise and temporary fluctuations

Unlike Fourier transform, wavelets preserve time information while analyzing frequencies.

2.2 Maximum Overlap Discrete Wavelet Transform (MODWT)

MODWT Decomposition

For a time series $(Z_t)$, MODWT produces:

$$\tilde{V}_{1,t} = \sum_{l=0}^{L-1} \tilde{g}_l Z_{t-l \text{ mod } T}$$ (Long-run component)

$$\tilde{W}_{1,t} = \sum_{l=0}^{L-1} \tilde{h}_l Z_{t-l \text{ mod } T}$$ (Short-run noise)

where $\tilde{g}$ and $\tilde{h}$ are low-pass and high-pass filters respectively.

Why MODWT over DWT?

  • No downsampling: Maintains original sample size
  • Translation invariant: Consistent results regardless of starting point
  • Better for real-time trading: Can update with new data

2.3 Application to Pairs Trading

Wavelet-Filtered Spread Construction

  1. Filter prices first: Apply MODWT to get $\tilde{V}_{i,1,t}$ and $\tilde{V}_{j,1,t}$
  2. Estimate parameters on filtered data: Get $\hat{\alpha}^w$ and $\hat{\beta}^w$
  3. Create filtered spread: $\hat{s}^w_t = \tilde{V}_{i,1,t} - \hat{\alpha}^w - \hat{\beta}^w\tilde{V}_{j,1,t}$
  4. Trade on original prices: Use filtered spread signals for actual trades

5.1 More Stationary Spreads

Wavelet filtering dramatically improves spread stationarity because:

  • Removes temporary noise that obscures true relationships
  • Parameters estimated on filtered data remain valid longer
  • More mean reversion → more successful trades

5.2 More Conservative Spreads

Spread Characteristics

Wavelet-filtered spreads have:

  • Lower means: Closer to zero (better centered)
  • Lower variance: More stable, less extreme deviations
  • Tighter thresholds: Earlier trade signals
  • Smaller deviations: When non-convergent, losses are smaller

5.3 Better Parameter Estimation

The Measurement Error Problem

With noisy prices: $S_{noisy} = S_{true} + \epsilon$

OLS estimation of $\beta$ is biased due to measurement error in both variables

Wavelet filtering removes high-frequency $\epsilon$, leading to:

  • More accurate $\hat{\beta}^w$ estimates
  • Better hedge ratios
  • Parameters that remain relevant in trading period

5.4 Noise Analysis

Principal Component Analysis of Filtered Noise

  • First PC explains 59-62% of variance: Strong common component
  • Negative correlation with returns: More noise → worse performance
  • Market-wide nature: Likely represents common market movements

This common noise causes spurious correlations and poor parameter estimates.

7. Practical Implementation Guide

7.1 Step-by-Step Process

Implementation Steps

  1. Data Preparation:
    • Collect daily adjusted prices for universe of stocks
    • Ensure continuous trading for training + trading period
  2. Wavelet Filtering:
    • Apply MODWT with sym22 (or highest available symlet)
    • Extract low-frequency component (Level 1)
    • Use symmetrization for boundary handling
  3. Pair Selection (Training Period):
    • Apply cointegration test or distance method on filtered prices
    • Select top pairs (e.g., 1000 for distance, all significant for coint)
  4. Parameter Estimation:
    • Estimate α and β on filtered prices
    • Calculate spread statistics (mean, std) on filtered spread
  5. Trading (Out-of-Sample):
    • Apply same wavelet filter to new prices
    • Calculate spread using pre-estimated parameters
    • Trade when |spread| > 2σ
    • Close when spread crosses zero

7.2 Key Considerations

Important Notes

  • Filter prices, not spreads: Apply wavelet to individual price series first
  • Trade on raw prices: Use filtered signals but execute on actual prices
  • Parameter stability: Re-estimate periodically (e.g., yearly)
  • Risk management: Set maximum loss limits for non-convergent trades

7.3 Computational Efficiency

The authors report their MATLAB implementation processes one pair in 0.78 seconds, allowing real-time application for 1000 pairs with updates every 13 minutes.

8. Broader Implications and Future Research

8.1 Why Standard Pairs Trading Has Been Failing

The Noise Problem

The paper reveals that increasing market noise has made traditional pairs trading unprofitable:

  • Common factors dominate: Market-wide movements mask pair relationships
  • False positives increase: Noise creates spurious correlations
  • Parameters unstable: Relationships estimated with noise don't persist
  • Negative correlation: Higher noise directly correlates with worse returns

8.2 Compatibility with Other Methods

Wavelet Filtering as a Complement

The wavelet approach can enhance other advanced methods:

  • Copula methods: Better marginal distributions
  • Machine learning: Cleaner features for models
  • Deep learning (LSTM): Less noisy training data
  • High-frequency trading: Multi-scale analysis possible

8.3 Limitations and Considerations

Important Limitations

  • Lookback bias: Need sufficient history for wavelet decomposition
  • Boundary effects: Recent data points affected by symmetrization
  • Computational overhead: Additional processing step required
  • Parameter selection: Choice of wavelet family and order matters

9. Conclusions

Main Takeaways

  1. Noise is the enemy: Common noise in stock prices has been destroying pairs trading profitability
  2. Wavelets provide the solution: Filtering separates signal from noise effectively
  3. Dramatic improvements: Returns improve by 11-12%, Sharpe ratios become strongly positive
  4. Mechanism is clear: Better parameter estimation and more stationary spreads
  5. Robust across methods: Works for both cointegration and distance approaches
  6. Practical and implementable: Computationally feasible for real-time trading

The Bottom Line

This paper provides a simple yet powerful solution to the declining profitability of pairs trading. By applying wavelet transform to filter out noise before pair selection and parameter estimation, traders can:

  • Transform losing strategies into highly profitable ones
  • Reduce risk while increasing returns
  • Make pairs trading viable again in modern markets

The technique is general enough to potentially improve other statistical arbitrage strategies that rely on estimating relationships between asset prices.
